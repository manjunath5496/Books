 <h2 class="content__headline">Ten books that changed the world</h2>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<ul>
<li><a class="u-underline" href="https://github.com/manjunath5496/Books/blob/master/book1.pdf" data-link-name="in body link"><strong>The Second Sex</strong></a><strong>&nbsp;:&nbsp;</strong><em>The Second Sex</em>&nbsp;(<a title="French language" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_language">French</a>:&nbsp;<em lang="fr">Le Deuxi&egrave;me Sexe</em>) is a 1949 book by the French&nbsp;<a title="Existentialism" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existentialism">existentialist</a>&nbsp;<a title="Simone de Beauvoir" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simone_de_Beauvoir">Simone de Beauvoir</a>, in which the author discusses the treatment of women throughout history. Beauvoir researched and wrote the book in about 14 months between 1946 and 1949.&nbsp;She published it in two volumes,&nbsp;<em>Facts and Myths</em>&nbsp;and&nbsp;<em>Lived Experience</em>&nbsp;(<em>Les faits et les mythes</em>&nbsp;and&nbsp;<em>L'exp&eacute;rience v&eacute;cue</em>&nbsp;in French). Some chapters first appeared in&nbsp;<em><a title="Les Temps modernes" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Les_Temps_modernes">Les Temps modernes</a></em>.&nbsp;One of Beauvoir's best-known books,&nbsp;<em>The Second Sex</em>&nbsp;is often regarded as a major work of feminist philosophy and the starting point of&nbsp;<a title="Second-wave feminism" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second-wave_feminism">second-wave feminism</a>.</li>
</ul>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<ul>
<li><strong><strong><a href="https://github.com/manjunath5496/Books/blob/master/book2.pdf">The Analects</a>:&nbsp;</strong></strong>The&nbsp;<em><strong>Analects</strong></em>&nbsp;(<a title="Traditional Chinese characters" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Traditional_Chinese_characters">Chinese</a>:&nbsp;<span lang="zh-Hant"><a class="extiw" title="wikt:" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E8%AB%96%E8%AA%9E"></a></span>;&nbsp;<a title="Pinyin" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pinyin">pinyin</a>:&nbsp;<em><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">L&uacute;ny</span></em>;&nbsp;<a title="Old Chinese" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Old_Chinese">Old Chinese</a>:&nbsp;<em>[r]u[n] (r)a</em>; meaning "Selected Sayings"), also known as the&nbsp;<em><strong>Analects of Confucius</strong></em>, is an ancient Chinese book composed of a collection of sayings and ideas attributed to the Chinese philosopher&nbsp;<a title="Confucius" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Confucius">Confucius</a>&nbsp;and his contemporaries, traditionally believed to have been compiled and written by Confucius's followers. It is believed to have been written during the&nbsp;<a title="Warring States period" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Warring_States_period">Warring States period</a>&nbsp;(475&ndash;221 BC), and it achieved its final form during the mid-<a title="Han dynasty" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Han_dynasty">Han dynasty</a>&nbsp;(206 BC&ndash;220 AD). By the early Han dynasty the&nbsp;<em>Analects</em>&nbsp;was considered merely a "commentary" on the&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Five Classics" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Five_Classics">Five Classics</a>, but the status of the&nbsp;<em>Analects</em>&nbsp;grew to be one of the central texts of&nbsp;<a title="Confucianism" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Confucianism">Confucianism</a>&nbsp;by the end of that dynasty. During the late&nbsp;<a title="Song dynasty" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Song_dynasty">Song dynasty</a>&nbsp;(960&ndash;1279) the importance of the&nbsp;<em>Analects</em>&nbsp;as a&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Philosophical" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philosophical">philosophy</a>&nbsp;work was raised above that of the older Five Classics, and it was recognized as one of the "<a class="mw-redirect" title="Four Books" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Four_Books">Four Books</a>". The&nbsp;<em>Analects</em>&nbsp;has been one of the most widely-read and studied books in China for the last 2,000 years, and continues to have a substantial influence on Chinese and&nbsp;<a title="Eastern philosophy" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eastern_philosophy#East_Asian_philosophies">East Asian thought and values</a>&nbsp;today. Confucius believed that the welfare of a country depended on the moral cultivation of its people, beginning from the nation's leadership. He believed that individuals could begin to cultivate an all-encompassing sense of virtue through&nbsp;<em><a title="Ren (Confucianism)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ren_(Confucianism)">ren</a></em>, and that the most basic step to cultivating&nbsp;<em>ren</em>&nbsp;was&nbsp;<a title="Filial piety" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Filial_piety">devotion to one's parents</a>&nbsp;and older siblings. He taught that one's individual desires do not need to be suppressed, but that people should be educated to reconcile their desires via&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Li (Confucian)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Li_(Confucian)">rituals and forms of propriety</a>, through which people could demonstrate their respect for others and their responsible roles in society. Confucius taught that a ruler's sense of&nbsp;<a title="De (Chinese)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/De_(Chinese)">virtue</a>&nbsp;was his primary prerequisite for leadership. His primary goal in educating his students was to produce&nbsp;<a title="Junzi" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Junzi">ethically well-cultivated men</a>&nbsp;who would carry themselves with gravity, speak correctly, and demonstrate consummate integrity in all things.</li>
</ul>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<ul>
<li><strong><strong><a href="https://github.com/manjunath5496/Books/blob/master/book3.pdf">The Origin of Species</a>:&nbsp;</strong></strong><em>On<strong> the Origin of Species</strong></em>&nbsp;(or, more completely,&nbsp;<em><strong>On the Origin of Species by Means of Natural Selection, or the Preservation of Favoured Races in the Struggle for Life</strong></em>),&nbsp;published on 24 November 1859, is a work of&nbsp;<a title="Scientific literature" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scientific_literature">scientific literature</a>&nbsp;by&nbsp;<a title="Charles Darwin" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_Darwin">Charles Darwin</a>&nbsp;which is considered to be the foundation of&nbsp;<a title="Evolutionary biology" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Evolutionary_biology">evolutionary biology</a>.&nbsp;Darwin's book introduced the&nbsp;<a title="Scientific theory" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scientific_theory">scientific theory</a>&nbsp;that populations&nbsp;<a title="Evolution" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Evolution">evolve</a>&nbsp;over the course of generations through a process of&nbsp;<a title="Natural selection" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_selection">natural selection</a>. It presented a body of evidence that&nbsp;<a title="Biodiversity" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity">the diversity of life</a>&nbsp;arose by&nbsp;<a title="Common descent" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Common_descent">common descent</a>&nbsp;through a&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Tree of life (science)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tree_of_life_(science)">branching pattern of evolution</a>. Darwin included evidence that he had gathered on&nbsp;<a title="Second voyage of HMS Beagle" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_voyage_of_HMS_Beagle">the&nbsp;<em>Beagle</em>&nbsp;expedition</a>&nbsp;in the 1830s and his subsequent findings from research, correspondence, and experimentation.&nbsp;<a title="History of evolutionary thought" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_evolutionary_thought">Various evolutionary ideas</a>&nbsp;had already been proposed to explain&nbsp;<a title="History of biology" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_biology">new findings in biology</a>. There was growing support for such ideas among dissident anatomists and the general public, but during the first half of the 19th century the English scientific establishment was closely tied to the&nbsp;<a title="Church of England" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Church_of_England">Church of England</a>, while science was part of&nbsp;<a title="Natural theology" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_theology">natural theology</a>. Ideas about the&nbsp;<a title="Transmutation of species" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transmutation_of_species">transmutation of species</a>&nbsp;were controversial as they conflicted with the beliefs that species were unchanging parts of a designed hierarchy and that humans were unique, unrelated to other animals. The political and theological implications were intensely debated, but transmutation was not accepted by the scientific mainstream. The book was written for non-specialist readers and attracted widespread interest upon its publication. As Darwin was an eminent scientist, his findings were taken seriously and the evidence he presented generated scientific, philosophical, and religious discussion. The debate over the book contributed to the campaign by&nbsp;<a title="Thomas Henry Huxley" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thomas_Henry_Huxley">T.&nbsp;H. Huxley</a>&nbsp;and his fellow members of the&nbsp;<a title="X Club" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X_Club">X Club</a>&nbsp;to&nbsp;<a title="Secularity" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Secularity">secularise</a>&nbsp;science by promoting&nbsp;<a title="Naturalism (philosophy)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naturalism_(philosophy)">scientific naturalism</a>. Within two decades there was widespread scientific agreement that evolution, with a branching pattern of common descent, had occurred, but scientists were slow to give natural selection the significance that Darwin thought appropriate. During "<a title="The eclipse of Darwinism" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_eclipse_of_Darwinism">the eclipse of Darwinism</a>" from the 1880s to the 1930s, various other mechanisms of evolution were given more credit. With the development of the&nbsp;<a title="Extended evolutionary synthesis" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extended_evolutionary_synthesis">modern evolutionary synthesis</a>&nbsp;in the 1930s and 1940s, Darwin's concept of evolutionary&nbsp;<a title="Adaptation" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adaptation">adaptation</a>&nbsp;through natural selection became central to modern evolutionary theory, and it has now become the unifying concept of the&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Life science" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life_science">life sciences</a>.</li>
</ul>

<ul>
<li><strong><a href="https://github.com/manjunath5496/Books/blob/master/book4.pdf">Elements</a>:&nbsp;</strong>The&nbsp;<em><strong>Elements</strong></em>&nbsp;(<a class="mw-redirect" title="Ancient Greek language" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greek_language">Ancient Greek</a>:&nbsp;<span lang="grc">&Sigma;&tau;&omicron;&iota;&chi;&epsilon;&alpha;</span>&nbsp;<em>Stoicheia</em>) is a&nbsp;<a title="Mathematics" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics">mathematical</a>&nbsp;<a title="Treatise" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Treatise">treatise</a>&nbsp;consisting of 13 books attributed to the ancient&nbsp;<a title="Greek mathematics" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greek_mathematics">Greek mathematician</a>&nbsp;<a title="Euclid" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid">Euclid</a>&nbsp;in&nbsp;<a title="Alexandria" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexandria">Alexandria</a>,&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Ptolemaic Egypt" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ptolemaic_Egypt">Ptolemaic Egypt</a>&nbsp;c. 300 BC. It is a collection of definitions, postulates, propositions (<a title="Theorem" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theorem">theorems</a>&nbsp;and&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Compass and straightedge constructions" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compass_and_straightedge_constructions">constructions</a>), and&nbsp;<a title="Mathematical proof" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_proof">mathematical proofs</a>&nbsp;of the propositions. The books cover plane and solid&nbsp;<a title="Euclidean geometry" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_geometry">Euclidean geometry</a>, elementary&nbsp;<a title="Number theory" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number_theory">number theory</a>, and&nbsp;<a title="Commensurability (mathematics)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commensurability_(mathematics)">incommensurable</a>&nbsp;lines.&nbsp;<em>Elements</em>&nbsp;is the oldest extant large-scale deductive treatment of&nbsp;<a title="Mathematics" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics">mathematics</a>. It has proven instrumental in the development of&nbsp;<a title="Logic" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logic">logic</a>&nbsp;and modern&nbsp;<a title="Science" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Science">science</a>, and its logical rigor was not surpassed until the 19th century. Euclid's&nbsp;<em>Elements</em>&nbsp;has been referred to as the most successful&nbsp;and influential textbook ever written. It was one of the very earliest mathematical works to be printed after the&nbsp;<a title="Movable type" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Movable_type">invention of the printing press</a>&nbsp;and has been estimated to be second only to the&nbsp;<a title="Bible" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bible">Bible</a>&nbsp;in the number of editions published since the first printing in 1482,&nbsp;with the number reaching well over one thousand.&nbsp;For centuries, when the&nbsp;<a title="Quadrivium" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrivium">quadrivium</a>&nbsp;was included in the curriculum of all university students, knowledge of at least part of Euclid's&nbsp;<em>Elements</em>&nbsp;was required of all students. Not until the 20th century, by which time its content was universally taught through other school textbooks, did it cease to be considered something all educated people had read. Geometry emerged as an indispensable part of the standard education of the English gentleman in the eighteenth century; by the&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Victorian period" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Victorian_period">Victorian period</a>&nbsp;it was also becoming an important part of the education of artisans, children at Board Schools, colonial subjects and, to a rather lesser degree, women. ... The standard textbook for this purpose was none other than Euclid's&nbsp;<em>The Elements</em>.&nbsp;</li>
</ul>

<li><strong><strong><a href="https://github.com/manjunath5496/Books/blob/master/book5.pdf">The Interpretation of Dreams</a>:&nbsp;</strong></strong><em>The<strong> Interpretation of Dreams</strong></em>&nbsp;(<a title="German language" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_language">German</a>:&nbsp;<em lang="de">Die Traumdeutung</em>) is an 1899 book by&nbsp;<a title="Sigmund Freud" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sigmund_Freud">Sigmund Freud</a>, the founder of&nbsp;<a title="Psychoanalysis" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Psychoanalysis">psychoanalysis</a>, in which the author introduces his theory of the&nbsp;<a title="Unconscious mind" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unconscious_mind">unconscious</a>&nbsp;with respect to&nbsp;<a title="Dream interpretation" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dream_interpretation">dream interpretation</a>, and discusses what would later become the theory of the&nbsp;<a title="Oedipus complex" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oedipus_complex">Oedipus complex</a>. Freud revised the book at least eight times and, in the third edition, added an extensive section which treated dream symbolism very literally, following the influence of&nbsp;<a title="Wilhelm Stekel" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilhelm_Stekel">Wilhelm Stekel</a>. Freud said of this work, "Insight such as this falls to one's lot but once in a lifetime." Dated 1900, the book was first published in an edition of 600 copies, which did not sell out for eight years.&nbsp;<em>The Interpretation of Dreams</em>&nbsp;later gained in popularity, and seven more editions were published in Freud's lifetime. Because of the book's length and complexity, Freud also wrote an abridged version called&nbsp;<em>On Dreams</em>. The original text is widely regarded as one of Freud's most significant works.</li>
</ul>

<li><strong><a href="https://github.com/manjunath5496/Books/blob/master/book6.pdf">A Sand County Almanac</a>:&nbsp;</strong><em>A<strong> Sand County Almanac: And Sketches Here and There</strong></em>&nbsp;is a 1949 non-fiction book by American&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Ecologist" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecologist">ecologist</a>, forester, and&nbsp;<a title="Environmentalist" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Environmentalist">environmentalist</a>&nbsp;<a title="Aldo Leopold" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aldo_Leopold">Aldo Leopold</a>. Describing the land around the&nbsp;<a title="Writer's home" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Writer%27s_home">author's home</a>&nbsp;in&nbsp;<a title="Sauk County, Wisconsin" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sauk_County,_Wisconsin">Sauk County, Wisconsin</a>, the collection of essays advocate Leopold's idea of a "<a title="Land ethic" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Land_ethic">land ethic</a>", or a responsible relationship existing between people and the land they inhabit. Edited and published by his son,&nbsp;<a title="Luna Leopold" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Luna_Leopold">Luna</a>, a year after Leopold's death, the book is considered a landmark in the American&nbsp;<a title="Conservation movement" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conservation_movement">conservation movement</a>. The book has had over two million copies printed and has been translated into at least fourteen languages.&nbsp;It has informed and changed the&nbsp;<a title="Environmental movement" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Environmental_movement">environmental movement</a>&nbsp;and stimulated a widespread interest in&nbsp;<a title="Ecology" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecology">ecology</a>&nbsp;as a science.</li>

<li><strong><a href="https://github.com/manjunath5496/Books/blob/master/book7.pdf">The Communist Manifesto</a>:&nbsp;</strong><em>The<strong> Communist Manifesto</strong></em>, originally the&nbsp;<em><strong>Manifesto of the Communist Party</strong></em>&nbsp;(<a title="German language" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_language">German</a>:&nbsp;<em lang="de">Manifest der Kommunistischen Partei</em>), is an 1848 political document by German philosophers&nbsp;<a title="Karl Marx" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karl_Marx">Karl Marx</a>&nbsp;and&nbsp;<a title="Friedrich Engels" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedrich_Engels">Friedrich Engels</a>. Commissioned by the&nbsp;<a title="Communist League" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Communist_League">Communist League</a>&nbsp;and originally published in&nbsp;<a title="London" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/London">London</a>&nbsp;just as the&nbsp;<a title="Revolutions of 1848" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Revolutions_of_1848">Revolutions of 1848</a>&nbsp;began to erupt, the&nbsp;<em>Manifesto</em>&nbsp;was later recognised as one of the world's most influential political documents. It presents an analytical approach to the&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Class struggle" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Class_struggle">class struggle</a>&nbsp;(historical and then-present) and the conflicts of&nbsp;<a title="Capitalism" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Capitalism">capitalism</a>&nbsp;and the&nbsp;<a title="Capitalist mode of production (Marxist theory)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Capitalist_mode_of_production_(Marxist_theory)">capitalist mode of production</a>, rather than a prediction of&nbsp;<a title="Communism" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Communism">communism</a>'s potential future forms.&nbsp;<em>The Communist Manifesto</em>&nbsp;summarises Marx and Engels' theories concerning the nature of society and politics, namely that in their own words "[t]he history of all hitherto existing society is the history of class struggles". It also briefly features their ideas for how the capitalist society of the time would eventually be replaced by&nbsp;<a title="Socialist mode of production" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socialist_mode_of_production">socialism</a>. In the last paragraph of the&nbsp;<em>Manifesto</em>, the authors call for a "forcible overthrow of all existing social conditions", which served as a call for communist revolutions around the world. In 2013,&nbsp;<em>The Communist Manifesto</em>&nbsp;was registered to&nbsp;<a title="UNESCO" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/UNESCO">UNESCO</a>'s&nbsp;<a title="Memory of the World Programme" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Memory_of_the_World_Programme">Memory of the World Programme</a>&nbsp;along with Marx's&nbsp;<em><a class="mw-redirect" title="Capital, Volume I" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Capital,_Volume_I">Capital, Volume I</a></em>.</li>

<li><strong><a class="u-underline" href="https://github.com/manjunath5496/Books/blob/master/book8.pdf" data-link-name="in body link">Beloved</a>:&nbsp;</strong><em><strong>Beloved</strong></em>&nbsp;is a 1987 novel by the American writer&nbsp;<a title="Toni Morrison" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toni_Morrison">Toni Morrison</a>. Set after the&nbsp;<a title="American Civil War" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_Civil_War">American Civil War</a>&nbsp;(1861&ndash;65), it is inspired by the life of&nbsp;<a title="Margaret Garner" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Margaret_Garner">Margaret Garner</a>, an African American who escaped slavery in&nbsp;<a title="Kentucky" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kentucky">Kentucky</a>&nbsp;in late January 1856 by crossing the&nbsp;<a title="Ohio River" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ohio_River">Ohio River</a>&nbsp;to&nbsp;<a title="Ohio" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ohio">Ohio</a>, a&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Slave and free states" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slave_and_free_states">free state</a>. Morrison had come across an account of Garner, "A Visit to the Slave Mother who Killed Her Child" in an 1856 newspaper article published in the&nbsp;<em>American Baptist</em>, and reproduced in&nbsp;<em>The Black Book</em>, a miscellaneous compilation of black history and culture that Morrison edited in 1974. The novel won the&nbsp;<a title="Pulitzer Prize for Fiction" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pulitzer_Prize_for_Fiction">Pulitzer Prize for Fiction</a>&nbsp;in 1988&nbsp;and was a finalist for the 1987&nbsp;<a title="National Book Award" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Book_Award">National Book Award</a>.&nbsp;It was adapted as a&nbsp;<a title="Beloved (1998 film)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beloved_(1998_film)">1998 movie of the same name</a>, starring&nbsp;<a title="Oprah Winfrey" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oprah_Winfrey">Oprah Winfrey</a>. A&nbsp;<em><a class="mw-redirect" title="New York Times" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_York_Times">New York Times</a></em>&nbsp;survey of writers and literary critics ranked it as the best work of American fiction from 1981 to 2006.</li>

<li><strong><a href="https://github.com/manjunath5496/Books/blob/master/book10.pdf">The Holy Bible</a>:&nbsp;</strong>The&nbsp;<strong>Bible</strong>&nbsp;(from&nbsp;<a title="Koine Greek" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Koine_Greek">Koine Greek</a>&nbsp;&tau; &beta;&iota;&beta;&lambda;&alpha;,&nbsp;<em>t&agrave;&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Bibl&iacute;a" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bibl%C3%ADa">bibl&iacute;a</a></em>, "the books")&nbsp;is a collection of&nbsp;<a title="Religious text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Religious_text">sacred texts</a>&nbsp;or scriptures. Varying parts of the Bible are considered to be a&nbsp;<a title="Biblical inspiration" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biblical_inspiration">product of divine inspiration</a>&nbsp;and a record of the relationship between&nbsp;<a title="God in Abrahamic religions" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/God_in_Abrahamic_religions">God</a>&nbsp;and&nbsp;<a title="Human" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human">humans</a>&nbsp;by&nbsp;<a title="Christians" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christians">Christians</a>,&nbsp;<a title="Jews" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jews">Jews</a>,&nbsp;<a title="Samaritanism" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Samaritanism">Samaritans</a>, and&nbsp;<a title="Rastafari" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rastafari">Rastafari</a>. The Bible appears in the form of an&nbsp;<a title="Anthology" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anthology">anthology</a>, a compilation of texts of a variety of forms that are all linked by the belief that they collectively contain the word of God. These texts include theologically-embellished historical accounts, hymns, allegorical erotica,&nbsp;<a title="Parable" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parable">parables</a>, and didactic letters. Those books included in the Bible by a tradition or group are called&nbsp;<a title="Biblical canon" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biblical_canon">canonical</a>, indicating that the tradition/group views the collection as the true representation of God's word and will. A number of Biblical canons have evolved, with overlapping and diverging contents from denomination to denomination.&nbsp;The&nbsp;<a title="Hebrew Bible" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hebrew_Bible">Hebrew Bible</a>&nbsp;overlaps with the Greek&nbsp;<a title="Septuagint" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Septuagint">Septuagint</a>&nbsp;and the Christian&nbsp;<a title="Old Testament" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Old_Testament">Old Testament</a>. The Christian&nbsp;<a title="New Testament" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_Testament">New Testament</a>&nbsp;is a collection of writings by&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Early Christians" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Early_Christians">early Christians</a>, believed to be mostly&nbsp;<a title="Jewish Christian" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jewish_Christian">Jewish disciples of Christ</a>, written in first-century&nbsp;<a title="Koine Greek" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Koine_Greek">Koine Greek</a>. Among&nbsp;<a title="Christian denomination" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christian_denomination">Christian denominations</a>&nbsp;there is some disagreement about what should be included in the canon, primarily about the&nbsp;<a title="Biblical apocrypha" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biblical_apocrypha">biblical apocrypha</a>, a list of works that are regarded with varying levels of respect. Attitudes towards the Bible also differ among Christian groups.&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholics" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_Catholics">Roman Catholics</a>,&nbsp;<a title="High church" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High_church">high church</a>&nbsp;<a title="Anglicanism" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anglicanism">Anglicans</a>,&nbsp;<a title="Methodism" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methodism">Methodists</a>&nbsp;and&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Eastern Orthodox" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eastern_Orthodox">Eastern Orthodox</a>&nbsp;Christians stress the harmony and importance of both the Bible and&nbsp;<a title="Sacred tradition" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sacred_tradition">sacred tradition</a>,&nbsp;while many&nbsp;<a title="Protestantism" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protestantism">Protestant</a>&nbsp;churches focus on the idea of&nbsp;<em><a title="Sola scriptura" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sola_scriptura">sola scriptura</a></em>, or scripture alone. This concept arose during the&nbsp;<a title="Reformation" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reformation">Reformation</a>, and many denominations today support the use of the Bible as the only&nbsp;<a title="Biblical infallibility" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biblical_infallibility">infallible</a>&nbsp;source of Christian teaching. Others though, advance the concept of&nbsp;<em><a title="Prima scriptura" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prima_scriptura">prima scriptura</a></em>&nbsp;in contrast. The Bible has been a massive influence on literature and history, especially in the&nbsp;<a title="Western world" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_world">Western world</a>, where the&nbsp;<a title="Gutenberg Bible" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gutenberg_Bible">Gutenberg Bible</a>&nbsp;was the first book printed using&nbsp;<a title="Movable type" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Movable_type">movable type</a>.&nbsp;According to the March 2007 edition of&nbsp;<em><a title="Time (magazine)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time_(magazine)">Time</a></em>, the Bible "has done more to shape literature, history, entertainment, and culture than any book ever written. Its influence on world history is unparalleled, and shows no signs of abating."&nbsp;With estimated total sales of over 5 billion copies, it is widely considered to be the most influential and&nbsp;<a title="List of best-selling books" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_best-selling_books">best-selling book of all time</a>.&nbsp;As of the 2000s, it sells approximately 100 million copies annually.</li>
</ul>
