 <h2 class="content__headline">Ten books that changed the world</h2>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<ul>
<li><a class="u-underline" href="https://github.com/manjunath5496/Books/blob/master/book1.pdf" data-link-name="in body link"><strong>The Second Sex</strong></a><strong>&nbsp;:&nbsp;</strong><em>The Second Sex</em>&nbsp;(<a title="French language" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_language">French</a>:&nbsp;<em lang="fr">Le Deuxi&egrave;me Sexe</em>) is a 1949 book by the French&nbsp;<a title="Existentialism" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existentialism">existentialist</a>&nbsp;<a title="Simone de Beauvoir" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simone_de_Beauvoir">Simone de Beauvoir</a>, in which the author discusses the treatment of women throughout history. Beauvoir researched and wrote the book in about 14 months between 1946 and 1949.&nbsp;She published it in two volumes,&nbsp;<em>Facts and Myths</em>&nbsp;and&nbsp;<em>Lived Experience</em>&nbsp;(<em>Les faits et les mythes</em>&nbsp;and&nbsp;<em>L'exp&eacute;rience v&eacute;cue</em>&nbsp;in French). Some chapters first appeared in&nbsp;<em><a title="Les Temps modernes" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Les_Temps_modernes">Les Temps modernes</a></em>.&nbsp;One of Beauvoir's best-known books,&nbsp;<em>The Second Sex</em>&nbsp;is often regarded as a major work of feminist philosophy and the starting point of&nbsp;<a title="Second-wave feminism" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second-wave_feminism">second-wave feminism</a>.</li>
</ul>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<ul>
<li><strong><strong><a href="https://github.com/manjunath5496/Books/blob/master/book2.pdf">The Analects</a>:&nbsp;</strong></strong>The&nbsp;<em><strong>Analects</strong></em>&nbsp;(<a title="Traditional Chinese characters" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Traditional_Chinese_characters">Chinese</a>:&nbsp;<span lang="zh-Hant"><a class="extiw" title="wikt:" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E8%AB%96%E8%AA%9E"></a></span>;&nbsp;<a title="Pinyin" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pinyin">pinyin</a>:&nbsp;<em><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">L&uacute;ny</span></em>;&nbsp;<a title="Old Chinese" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Old_Chinese">Old Chinese</a>:&nbsp;<em>[r]u[n] (r)a</em>; meaning "Selected Sayings"), also known as the&nbsp;<em><strong>Analects of Confucius</strong></em>, is an ancient Chinese book composed of a collection of sayings and ideas attributed to the Chinese philosopher&nbsp;<a title="Confucius" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Confucius">Confucius</a>&nbsp;and his contemporaries, traditionally believed to have been compiled and written by Confucius's followers. It is believed to have been written during the&nbsp;<a title="Warring States period" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Warring_States_period">Warring States period</a>&nbsp;(475&ndash;221 BC), and it achieved its final form during the mid-<a title="Han dynasty" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Han_dynasty">Han dynasty</a>&nbsp;(206 BC&ndash;220 AD). By the early Han dynasty the&nbsp;<em>Analects</em>&nbsp;was considered merely a "commentary" on the&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Five Classics" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Five_Classics">Five Classics</a>, but the status of the&nbsp;<em>Analects</em>&nbsp;grew to be one of the central texts of&nbsp;<a title="Confucianism" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Confucianism">Confucianism</a>&nbsp;by the end of that dynasty. During the late&nbsp;<a title="Song dynasty" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Song_dynasty">Song dynasty</a>&nbsp;(960&ndash;1279) the importance of the&nbsp;<em>Analects</em>&nbsp;as a&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Philosophical" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philosophical">philosophy</a>&nbsp;work was raised above that of the older Five Classics, and it was recognized as one of the "<a class="mw-redirect" title="Four Books" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Four_Books">Four Books</a>". The&nbsp;<em>Analects</em>&nbsp;has been one of the most widely-read and studied books in China for the last 2,000 years, and continues to have a substantial influence on Chinese and&nbsp;<a title="Eastern philosophy" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eastern_philosophy#East_Asian_philosophies">East Asian thought and values</a>&nbsp;today. Confucius believed that the welfare of a country depended on the moral cultivation of its people, beginning from the nation's leadership. He believed that individuals could begin to cultivate an all-encompassing sense of virtue through&nbsp;<em><a title="Ren (Confucianism)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ren_(Confucianism)">ren</a></em>, and that the most basic step to cultivating&nbsp;<em>ren</em>&nbsp;was&nbsp;<a title="Filial piety" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Filial_piety">devotion to one's parents</a>&nbsp;and older siblings. He taught that one's individual desires do not need to be suppressed, but that people should be educated to reconcile their desires via&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Li (Confucian)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Li_(Confucian)">rituals and forms of propriety</a>, through which people could demonstrate their respect for others and their responsible roles in society. Confucius taught that a ruler's sense of&nbsp;<a title="De (Chinese)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/De_(Chinese)">virtue</a>&nbsp;was his primary prerequisite for leadership. His primary goal in educating his students was to produce&nbsp;<a title="Junzi" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Junzi">ethically well-cultivated men</a>&nbsp;who would carry themselves with gravity, speak correctly, and demonstrate consummate integrity in all things.</li>
</ul>

<ul>
<li><strong><strong><a href="https://github.com/manjunath5496/Books/blob/master/book3.pdf">The Origin of Species</a>:&nbsp;</strong></strong><em>On<strong> the Origin of Species</strong></em>&nbsp;(or, more completely,&nbsp;<em><strong>On the Origin of Species by Means of Natural Selection, or the Preservation of Favoured Races in the Struggle for Life</strong></em>),&nbsp;published on 24 November 1859, is a work of&nbsp;<a title="Scientific literature" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scientific_literature">scientific literature</a>&nbsp;by&nbsp;<a title="Charles Darwin" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_Darwin">Charles Darwin</a>&nbsp;which is considered to be the foundation of&nbsp;<a title="Evolutionary biology" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Evolutionary_biology">evolutionary biology</a>.&nbsp;Darwin's book introduced the&nbsp;<a title="Scientific theory" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scientific_theory">scientific theory</a>&nbsp;that populations&nbsp;<a title="Evolution" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Evolution">evolve</a>&nbsp;over the course of generations through a process of&nbsp;<a title="Natural selection" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_selection">natural selection</a>. It presented a body of evidence that&nbsp;<a title="Biodiversity" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity">the diversity of life</a>&nbsp;arose by&nbsp;<a title="Common descent" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Common_descent">common descent</a>&nbsp;through a&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Tree of life (science)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tree_of_life_(science)">branching pattern of evolution</a>. Darwin included evidence that he had gathered on&nbsp;<a title="Second voyage of HMS Beagle" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_voyage_of_HMS_Beagle">the&nbsp;<em>Beagle</em>&nbsp;expedition</a>&nbsp;in the 1830s and his subsequent findings from research, correspondence, and experimentation.&nbsp;<a title="History of evolutionary thought" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_evolutionary_thought">Various evolutionary ideas</a>&nbsp;had already been proposed to explain&nbsp;<a title="History of biology" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_biology">new findings in biology</a>. There was growing support for such ideas among dissident anatomists and the general public, but during the first half of the 19th century the English scientific establishment was closely tied to the&nbsp;<a title="Church of England" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Church_of_England">Church of England</a>, while science was part of&nbsp;<a title="Natural theology" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_theology">natural theology</a>. Ideas about the&nbsp;<a title="Transmutation of species" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transmutation_of_species">transmutation of species</a>&nbsp;were controversial as they conflicted with the beliefs that species were unchanging parts of a designed hierarchy and that humans were unique, unrelated to other animals. The political and theological implications were intensely debated, but transmutation was not accepted by the scientific mainstream. The book was written for non-specialist readers and attracted widespread interest upon its publication. As Darwin was an eminent scientist, his findings were taken seriously and the evidence he presented generated scientific, philosophical, and religious discussion. The debate over the book contributed to the campaign by&nbsp;<a title="Thomas Henry Huxley" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thomas_Henry_Huxley">T.&nbsp;H. Huxley</a>&nbsp;and his fellow members of the&nbsp;<a title="X Club" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X_Club">X Club</a>&nbsp;to&nbsp;<a title="Secularity" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Secularity">secularise</a>&nbsp;science by promoting&nbsp;<a title="Naturalism (philosophy)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naturalism_(philosophy)">scientific naturalism</a>. Within two decades there was widespread scientific agreement that evolution, with a branching pattern of common descent, had occurred, but scientists were slow to give natural selection the significance that Darwin thought appropriate. During "<a title="The eclipse of Darwinism" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_eclipse_of_Darwinism">the eclipse of Darwinism</a>" from the 1880s to the 1930s, various other mechanisms of evolution were given more credit. With the development of the&nbsp;<a title="Extended evolutionary synthesis" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extended_evolutionary_synthesis">modern evolutionary synthesis</a>&nbsp;in the 1930s and 1940s, Darwin's concept of evolutionary&nbsp;<a title="Adaptation" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adaptation">adaptation</a>&nbsp;through natural selection became central to modern evolutionary theory, and it has now become the unifying concept of the&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Life science" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life_science">life sciences</a>.</li>
</ul>

<ul>
<li><strong><a href="https://github.com/manjunath5496/Books/blob/master/book4.pdf">Elements</a>:&nbsp;</strong>The&nbsp;<em><strong>Elements</strong></em>&nbsp;(<a class="mw-redirect" title="Ancient Greek language" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greek_language">Ancient Greek</a>:&nbsp;<span lang="grc">&Sigma;&tau;&omicron;&iota;&chi;&epsilon;&alpha;</span>&nbsp;<em>Stoicheia</em>) is a&nbsp;<a title="Mathematics" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics">mathematical</a>&nbsp;<a title="Treatise" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Treatise">treatise</a>&nbsp;consisting of 13 books attributed to the ancient&nbsp;<a title="Greek mathematics" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greek_mathematics">Greek mathematician</a>&nbsp;<a title="Euclid" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid">Euclid</a>&nbsp;in&nbsp;<a title="Alexandria" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexandria">Alexandria</a>,&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Ptolemaic Egypt" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ptolemaic_Egypt">Ptolemaic Egypt</a>&nbsp;c. 300 BC. It is a collection of definitions, postulates, propositions (<a title="Theorem" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theorem">theorems</a>&nbsp;and&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Compass and straightedge constructions" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compass_and_straightedge_constructions">constructions</a>), and&nbsp;<a title="Mathematical proof" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_proof">mathematical proofs</a>&nbsp;of the propositions. The books cover plane and solid&nbsp;<a title="Euclidean geometry" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_geometry">Euclidean geometry</a>, elementary&nbsp;<a title="Number theory" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number_theory">number theory</a>, and&nbsp;<a title="Commensurability (mathematics)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commensurability_(mathematics)">incommensurable</a>&nbsp;lines.&nbsp;<em>Elements</em>&nbsp;is the oldest extant large-scale deductive treatment of&nbsp;<a title="Mathematics" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics">mathematics</a>. It has proven instrumental in the development of&nbsp;<a title="Logic" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logic">logic</a>&nbsp;and modern&nbsp;<a title="Science" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Science">science</a>, and its logical rigor was not surpassed until the 19th century. Euclid's&nbsp;<em>Elements</em>&nbsp;has been referred to as the most successful&nbsp;and influential textbook ever written. It was one of the very earliest mathematical works to be printed after the&nbsp;<a title="Movable type" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Movable_type">invention of the printing press</a>&nbsp;and has been estimated to be second only to the&nbsp;<a title="Bible" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bible">Bible</a>&nbsp;in the number of editions published since the first printing in 1482,&nbsp;with the number reaching well over one thousand.&nbsp;For centuries, when the&nbsp;<a title="Quadrivium" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrivium">quadrivium</a>&nbsp;was included in the curriculum of all university students, knowledge of at least part of Euclid's&nbsp;<em>Elements</em>&nbsp;was required of all students. Not until the 20th century, by which time its content was universally taught through other school textbooks, did it cease to be considered something all educated people had read. Geometry emerged as an indispensable part of the standard education of the English gentleman in the eighteenth century; by the&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Victorian period" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Victorian_period">Victorian period</a>&nbsp;it was also becoming an important part of the education of artisans, children at Board Schools, colonial subjects and, to a rather lesser degree, women. ... The standard textbook for this purpose was none other than Euclid's&nbsp;<em>The Elements</em>.&nbsp;</li>

<li><strong><strong><a href="https://github.com/manjunath5496/Books/blob/master/book5.pdf">The Interpretation of Dreams</a>:&nbsp;</strong></strong><em>The<strong> Interpretation of Dreams</strong></em>&nbsp;(<a title="German language" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_language">German</a>:&nbsp;<em lang="de">Die Traumdeutung</em>) is an 1899 book by&nbsp;<a title="Sigmund Freud" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sigmund_Freud">Sigmund Freud</a>, the founder of&nbsp;<a title="Psychoanalysis" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Psychoanalysis">psychoanalysis</a>, in which the author introduces his theory of the&nbsp;<a title="Unconscious mind" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unconscious_mind">unconscious</a>&nbsp;with respect to&nbsp;<a title="Dream interpretation" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dream_interpretation">dream interpretation</a>, and discusses what would later become the theory of the&nbsp;<a title="Oedipus complex" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oedipus_complex">Oedipus complex</a>.
